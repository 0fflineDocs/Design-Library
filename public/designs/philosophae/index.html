Book I

§ Ancient Philosophy

c. 600 bce — 529 ce

Argument of the Book The era opens at Miletus, where Thales is said to have held that all things are water, and closes with Justinian's edict shuttering the Academy at Athens after nine centuries of operation. Between these two dates the Greeks invent, almost from nothing, the disciplines of metaphysics, ethics, politics, logic, natural philosophy, and aesthetics — and the Romans translate the inheritance into Latin, where it will wait out the long medieval custody of the Church.

Plate I
Plato of Athens — open entry
c. 428 — 348 bce · Athens · Founder of the Academy
Pupil of Socrates · Teacher of Aristotle · Foundational figure of Platonism

Born into the Athenian aristocracy in the generation following the Periclean age, Plato turned from a political career to the philosophical life under the influence of Socrates, whose trial and execution in 399 bce furnished the perpetual subject of his early dialogues. He founded the Academy outside Athens around 387 bce — the first sustained institution of higher learning in the Western world, where it would operate, with interruptions, until 529 ce.

The mature metaphysics holds that the visible world is a deficient copy of an intelligible realm of Forms (εἴδη) — eternal, unchanging archetypes of which particular things are imperfect instances. The Republic uses this doctrine to construct a tripartite soul (reason, spirit, appetite) and a just city ruled by philosopher-kings; the late dialogues — Parmenides, Sophist, Theaetetus, Timaeus — interrogate the Forms doctrine and pursue questions of being, knowledge, and cosmology with progressively more austere argument.

Principal Works
Apology·Phaedo·Symposium·Republic·Phaedrus·Theaetetus·Parmenides·Timaeus·Sophist·Laws
Lineage
Inherits the elenctic method from Socrates; engages with Parmenides and the Pythagoreans; bequeaths the school to Speusippus, then through the Middle and Neo-Platonists to Plotinus and, by long inheritance, to Augustine.
§ Further Reading

The Platonic corpus survives nearly intact — an exceptional fortune in classical letters — in thirty-six works traditionally arranged into nine tetralogies by Thrasyllus of Alexandria in the first century. Authenticity of several minor dialogues (Alcibiades I, Hippias Major, Theages) remains disputed by modern scholarship.

The so-called Tübingen School argues that Plato's deepest doctrine was reserved for oral teaching within the Academy and only obliquely indicated in the dialogues — the “unwritten doctrines” reported by Aristotle in Metaphysics A.6.

c. 428 bce, Athens or Aegina
Died
c. 348 bce, Athens

§ Schools of the Polis

Stoicism

3rd c. bce — 3rd c. ce

Founded by Zeno of Citium in the Stoa Poikilē at Athens. The Stoics held that virtue is the sole good, that the cosmos is governed by a rational logos with which the wise man aligns his will, and that destructive passions arise from false judgments about value.

Figures Zeno · Cleanthes · Chrysippus · Seneca · Epictetus · Marcus Aurelius

Epicureanism

307 bce — 4th c. ce

Epicurus taught from a walled garden outside Athens that pleasure (hēdonē) — properly understood as the absence of bodily pain (aponia) and mental disturbance (ataraxia) — is the end of life, and that the gods, though they exist, take no interest in human affairs.

Figures Epicurus · Metrodorus · Philodemus · Lucretius

Aristotelianism & the Peripatos

335 bce — 6th c. ce

Aristotle's school at the Lyceum advanced a systematic philosophy uniting logic, biology, ethics, politics, and metaphysics. Where Plato sought reality in transcendent Forms, Aristotle located it in the immanent ousia of concrete substances — form and matter inseparable.

Figures Aristotle · Theophrastus · Strato · Alexander of Aphrodisias

Pyrrhonian Skepticism

3rd c. bce — 3rd c. ce

Pyrrho of Elis returned from Alexander's Indian campaign teaching the suspension of judgment (epochē) on all matters of speculative belief — for every argument an opposing argument of equal force can be produced, and from this equipollence follows tranquility (ataraxia).

Figures Pyrrho · Timon · Aenesidemus · Sextus Empiricus

Neoplatonism

3rd c. — 6th c. ce

Plotinus reconstructed Plato into a hierarchical metaphysics descending from the ineffable One, through Intellect (Nous) and Soul, to the material world. The system would shape Christian, Jewish, and Islamic mysticism for a thousand years.

Figures Plotinus · Porphyry · Iamblichus · Proclus

The Pre-Socratics

6th — 5th c. bce

The Milesians (Thales, Anaximander, Anaximenes) sought a single archē underlying nature; Heraclitus countered with universal flux and the logos; Parmenides denied change altogether; Democritus reduced everything to atoms in the void.

Figures Thales · Heraclitus · Parmenides · Empedocles · Anaxagoras · Democritus
§ Key Concepts
The Forms
εἴδη · ideai

Eternal, unchanging archetypes of which sensible particulars are imperfect copies; the proper objects of true knowledge.

Eudaimonia
εὐδαιμονία

Human flourishing — the active life lived in accordance with virtue, identified by Aristotle as the highest good (telos).

Logos
λόγος

Reasoned account, ratio, the rational principle ordering the cosmos. Heraclitean in origin, central to Stoicism and later Christian theology.

Aporia
ἀπορία · puzzlement

The state of impasse the Socratic interlocutor arrives at when his confident definitions have been dismantled — the necessary opening of inquiry.

Phronēsis
φρόνησις

Practical wisdom — the deliberative virtue by which one perceives what right action consists in for the particular case at hand.

Ataraxia
ἀταραξία

Tranquility of mind, the goal both of the Epicurean (through right desire) and the Pyrrhonist (through suspension of judgment).

Book II

§ Medieval Philosophy

c. 400 — 1500 ce

Argument of the Book For a millennium, philosophy serves theology — first in the Latin West through Augustine's Christian Platonism, then through the Islamic recovery of Aristotle in tenth-century Baghdad, Cordoba and Cairo, and at last in the great thirteenth-century synthesis of Paris, where Aquinas reconciles the Stagirite with revelation. The age closes with the nominalist solvent of Ockham and the humanist return ad fontes.

Plate II
Thomas Aquinas — open entry
1225 — 1274 · Roccasecca · Paris · Naples · Doctor Angelicus
Dominican friar · Pupil of Albertus Magnus · Master of Theology, University of Paris

Born in the kingdom of Sicily to a family of minor nobility, Aquinas entered the Dominican order against his family's wishes — they imprisoned him for a year in an effort to dissuade him — and studied under Albertus Magnus at Cologne. His vocation was the integration of the newly recovered corpus of Aristotle, transmitted to Latin Christendom through Arabic commentary, with the doctrine of the Church.

The Summa Theologiae, composed between 1265 and his death, organises Christian doctrine as a single deductive system in three Parts and over five hundred questions. Each question is debated in the disputational form of the schools — objections, a settled response (respondeo), and replies. The five proofs of God's existence (the Quinque Viae) of i.q.2.a.3 remain the most-discussed pages of medieval thought.

Principal Works
Summa contra Gentiles·Summa Theologiae·De Ente et Essentia·Quaestiones Disputatae de Veritate· Commentaries on Aristotle & the Sentences
Lineage
Reads Aristotle through Averroes and Avicenna; opposes the Latin Averroists at Paris; canonised 1323; declared Doctor of the Church 1567. Thomism remains the official philosophy of Catholic teaching.
§ Further Reading

Aquinas distinguishes — against Averroes — between essence (essentia) and existence (esse) in all creatures, a distinction that becomes the metaphysical signature of his system. Only in God do essence and existence coincide; this is what it means for God to be ipsum esse subsistens, subsistent being itself.

The mystical experience of December 1273 — after which he abandoned the unfinished Summa, calling all he had written “straw” — has been a perennial subject for biographers.

Died

§ Scholastic and Falsafa Traditions

Augustinianism

5th — 13th c.

Augustine of Hippo synthesized Pauline Christianity with Neoplatonism, producing a theology of grace, original sin, and the inwardness of the soul that would govern Western piety and self-examination from the Confessions onward.

Figures Augustine · Boethius · Anselm · Bonaventure

Falsafa — Islamic Aristotelianism

9th — 12th c.

From Baghdad to Cordoba, philosophers in the Arabic tradition preserved, commented upon, and extended Aristotle and the Neoplatonists — the channel through which the corpus returned to Latin Europe in the twelfth century.

Figures al-Kindī · al-Fārābī · Ibn Sīnā (Avicenna) · al-Ghazālī · Ibn Rushd (Averroes)

The High Scholastics

13th c.

At Paris and Oxford the universities institutionalised the disputational method — the quaestio, the lectio, the commentary on the Sentences of Peter Lombard — producing the most systematic philosophical writing of the Christian Middle Ages.

Figures Albertus Magnus · Aquinas · Bonaventure · Duns Scotus · Roger Bacon

Nominalism

14th c.

William of Ockham denied that universals possess any reality outside the mind — they are mere names (nomina) for collections of particulars. The methodological consequence is the celebrated razor: entities are not to be multiplied without necessity.

Figures William of Ockham · Jean Buridan · Nicholas of Autrecourt
§ Key Concepts
Quinque Viae
five ways

Aquinas's five demonstrations of God's existence — from motion, causation, contingency, gradation, and final cause.

Aseity
aseitas · self-from

The divine property of existing of and from oneself, owing existence to nothing external — predicated of God alone.

Haecceitas
thisness

Duns Scotus's principle of individuation: that by which an entity is this one rather than any other, irreducible to its general nature.

Analogia Entis
analogy of being

The Thomist doctrine that “being” is predicated of God and creatures neither univocally nor equivocally, but by proportional analogy.

Ockham's Razor
novacula Occami

Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem — a methodological preference for the explanation with the fewest entities.

Universals
universalia

Whether humanity, redness, justice exist in themselves (realism), only in things (moderate realism), or only as names (nominalism) — the central medieval dispute.

Book III

§ Early Modern Philosophy

1600 — 1800

Argument of the Book With Descartes the medieval scaffolding falls away. Philosophy now begins not with the world or with God but with the thinking subject; the problem of the era becomes the problem of knowledge — what the mind can be certain of, and on what basis. The Continental rationalists place reason at the foundation, the British empiricists experience; Kant, at the close, proposes a critical synthesis that organises the question for a century to come.

Plate III
Immanuel Kant — open entry
1724 — 1804 · Königsberg · Professor of Logic and Metaphysics
Critical philosophy · Transcendental idealism · Initiator of German Idealism

Kant passed the whole of his life in the Prussian city of Königsberg, where his daily walk past the houses of his neighbours was said to be punctual enough for them to set their clocks by. The intellectual life, by contrast, was anything but settled: read by Hume in middle age, Kant claimed to have been awakened from his “dogmatic slumber” — the slumber, that is, of Wolffian rationalism.

The Critique of Pure Reason (1781, second edition 1787) executes a “Copernican revolution” in philosophy: knowledge does not conform to objects, but objects to the conditions of knowledge supplied by the mind. Space and time are the a priori forms of sensible intuition; the categories (substance, causation, &c.) are the forms of the understanding. We can know phenomena — appearances thus structured — but not noumena, things in themselves.

Principal Works
Critique of Pure Reason·Prolegomena·Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals·Critique of Practical Reason·Critique of the Power of Judgment·Religion within the Bounds of Mere Reason·Perpetual Peace
Lineage
Awakened by Hume; inheritor of Leibniz and Wolff; teacher (by reading) of Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, Schopenhauer; the unavoidable interlocutor of every subsequent metaphysician.
§ Further Reading

The second Critique grounds morality in the categorical imperative: act only on that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law. Ethics is thus made independent of consequence, inclination, and divine command — bound only to the rational will of the autonomous agent.

The third Critique unites theoretical and practical reason through the faculty of judgment, treating the beautiful, the sublime, and natural teleology — and quietly inaugurating modern aesthetics as a philosophical discipline.

§ Rationalism, Empiricism & the Enlightenment

Continental Rationalism

17th c.

Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz held that reason, working from clear and distinct ideas innate to the mind, can attain substantive truth about reality without dependence upon the senses. The geometric method of demonstration furnished the model.

Figures Descartes · Spinoza · Leibniz · Malebranche

British Empiricism

17th — 18th c.

Locke, Berkeley, and Hume held that the mind is at birth a tabula rasa, and that all the materials of thought are furnished by experience. Hume's critique of causation pressed the principle to its skeptical conclusion.

Figures Locke · Berkeley · Hume · Reid

Political Contractarianism

17th — 18th c.

Hobbes, Locke, and Rousseau derived legitimate political authority from the consent of the governed — a hypothetical contract among individuals in a pre-political “state of nature” whose terms each gave very differently.

Figures Hobbes · Locke · Rousseau · Montesquieu

Scottish Enlightenment

18th c.

Edinburgh and Glasgow produced, in a single generation, a science of human nature, a moral philosophy of sympathy, and the first systematic account of commercial society as a moral order.

Figures Hume · Adam Smith · Thomas Reid · Adam Ferguson
§ Key Concepts
Cogito
cogito, ergo sum

Descartes's indubitable starting point: even the most radical doubt presupposes a doubter, hence a thinking thing.

Tabula Rasa
blank slate

Locke's image of the mind at birth — devoid of innate ideas, written upon by experience alone.

Monad
monas · unity

Leibniz's elementary substance: simple, unextended, windowless, each mirroring the entire universe from its own point of view.

Categorical Imperative
kategorischer Imperativ

Kant's supreme moral law: act only on that maxim by which you can at the same time will it to become a universal law.

A Priori / A Posteriori
before · after experience

The distinction between what may be known independently of experience and what depends upon it — fundamental to all post-Kantian epistemology.

Sub Specie Aeternitatis
under the aspect of eternity

Spinoza's standpoint of philosophical contemplation: viewing things as the rational order of nature requires, not as the passions disclose them.

Book IV

§ The Nineteenth Century

1800 — 1900

Argument of the Book The century begins with Hegel completing the Kantian project on the largest possible scale — Spirit unfolding itself dialectically through history — and ends with Nietzsche announcing the death of God and the exhaustion of every system the German universities had built. Between these poles: Marx inverts Hegel into materialism; Kierkegaard rejects the system for the existing individual; Schopenhauer relocates reality in blind Will; the utilitarians remake ethics on the basis of consequence.

Plate IV
Friedrich Nietzsche — open entry
1844 — 1900 · Röcken · Basel · Sils-Maria · Weimar · Philologist · Philosopher
Post-Schopenhauer · Critic of morality and metaphysics · Forerunner of existentialism & postmodernism

Appointed Professor of Classical Philology at Basel at the age of twenty-four, Nietzsche resigned the chair a decade later in ill health and spent the productive remainder of his life as a wandering writer, summering at Sils-Maria in the Engadin and wintering in Genoa, Nice, and Turin. In January 1889 he suffered a mental collapse in the streets of Turin from which he never recovered.

Across The Genealogy of Morals, Beyond Good and Evil, and the prophetic Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Nietzsche prosecutes Christian and Platonic morality as the historical product of ressentiment — the slave revolt by which the weak inverted the noble values of the strong. The doctrines of the Übermensch, the eternal recurrence, and the will to power sketch the affirmative project that the critique was meant to clear ground for.

Principal Works
The Birth of Tragedy·Untimely Meditations·Human, All Too Human·The Gay Science·Thus Spoke Zarathustra·Beyond Good and Evil·On the Genealogy of Morals·Twilight of the Idols·Ecce Homo
Lineage
Awakened by Schopenhauer; close engagement and break with Wagner; ancestor of Heidegger, Foucault, Deleuze; misappropriated by his sister and the Nazi regime, rehabilitated by Walter Kaufmann in the post-war reception.
§ Further Reading

The eternal recurrence is presented in The Gay Science §341 as a thought-experiment: imagine that every detail of your life, in its full uneventfulness, must repeat itself eternally. Would you curse the demon who announced it, or would you find him divine? The question, for Nietzsche, is the touchstone of affirmation.

The unfinished “magnum opus,” The Will to Power, was assembled posthumously from Nachlass by his sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche and is now regarded as an editorial artefact rather than a Nietzschean work.

§ Idealism, Critique & Reaction

German Idealism

1781 — 1831

Fichte, Schelling, and Hegel pressed Kant's critical philosophy beyond its self-imposed limits toward a single absolute Idea that develops itself dialectically through nature, history, and Spirit (Geist) — culminating in Hegel's encyclopaedic system.

Figures Fichte · Schelling · Hegel · Schleiermacher

Utilitarianism

late 18th — 19th c.

Bentham and Mill held that the morality of an action is determined by its consequences for the aggregate happiness — “the greatest happiness of the greatest number” — providing the philosophical foundation of nineteenth-century reform.

Figures Bentham · J. S. Mill · Henry Sidgwick · Harriet Taylor Mill

Marxism

from 1848

Marx inverts Hegel's idealist dialectic into a materialist one: the engine of history is class struggle, the structure of consciousness is conditioned by the mode of production, and philosophy's task is no longer to interpret the world but to change it.

Figures Marx · Engels · later — Lenin, Lukács, Gramsci

Proto-Existentialism

mid 19th c.

Kierkegaard, writing under a battery of pseudonyms in Copenhagen, opposed the Hegelian system in the name of the existing individual, the leap of faith, and the irreducibility of subjective truth — themes the twentieth century would take up in earnest.

Figures Kierkegaard · Schopenhauer · Dostoevsky · the late Nietzsche
§ Key Concepts
Dialectic
Dialektik

Hegel's pattern of conceptual development: a thesis generates its negation, the two are taken up (aufgehoben) in a higher unity.

Geist
spirit · mind

The Hegelian subject of history: self-consciousness coming to know itself through its own objectifications in art, religion, and philosophy.

Übermensch
overman

The future human Nietzsche prophesies in Zarathustra: one who creates values out of the resources of life itself, beyond good and evil.

Will to Power
Wille zur Macht

For the later Nietzsche, the fundamental drive of all life — not for self-preservation but for the discharge and expansion of force.

Alienation
Entfremdung

For Marx, the estrangement of the worker from the product of labour, the act of labour, the species-being, and from other workers under capitalism.

Leap of Faith
Troens spring

Kierkegaard's name for the movement by which the existing individual relates absolutely to the absolute — undertaken in the absence of objective certainty.

Book V

§ Twentieth Century & Contemporary

1900 — the Present

Argument of the Book Philosophy in the twentieth century divides into two great rivers that for a long time would not acknowledge one another. On the continent, Husserl's phenomenology gives rise to Heidegger, Sartre, and Merleau-Ponty, then to structuralism and its successors. In the Anglosphere, Russell and Moore turn from idealism to analysis; the logical positivists attempt to put metaphysics out of business; Wittgenstein appears on both sides of the question, and changes his mind.

Plate V
Martin Heidegger — open entry
1889 — 1976 · Meßkirch · Freiburg · Todtnauberg · Rector of Freiburg, 1933
Phenomenology (after Husserl) · Existential analytic · The history of Being

Trained for the priesthood and turned by his reading of Brentano and the Greeks toward philosophy, Heidegger became Husserl's assistant at Freiburg and his successor in the chair of phenomenology. He passed almost his entire teaching life in the Black Forest of his upbringing, where he kept a hut at Todtnauberg in which much of his later writing was composed.

Sein und Zeit (1927) proposes to renew the question of the meaning of Being (Sein) — a question, Heidegger argues, that has been forgotten since the Greeks. The route to it lies through an analytic of the kind of being that can raise the question of being: human existence, named Dasein (“being-there”). The book's twin themes are being-in-the-world and being-toward-death.

Principal Works
Being and Time·What is Metaphysics?·The Origin of the Work of Art·Letter on Humanism·Contributions to Philosophy·The Question Concerning Technology·On the Way to Language
Lineage
Pupil of Husserl; teacher of Arendt, Gadamer, Marcuse, Levinas; an inescapable presence for Sartre, Merleau-Ponty, Derrida. His rectorship under National Socialism in 1933–34, and the postwar silence about it, remain matters of intense biographical and philosophical reckoning.
§ Further Reading

The later Heidegger — after the “turn” (die Kehre) — moves from the existential analytic toward what he calls the “history of being,” reading the Western philosophical tradition from Plato to Nietzsche as the progressive forgetting of Being and the rise of technology as its consummation.

The publication of the Schwarze Hefte (Black Notebooks) beginning in 2014 has reopened the question of antisemitic strains in Heidegger's thinking, and the relation between his philosophy and his politics is now an active subject of scholarship rather than a closed one.

§ Phenomenology, Analytic Philosophy & their Heirs

Phenomenology

from 1900

Husserl turned philosophy toward the descriptive analysis of consciousness as it presents itself — “to the things themselves” — by bracketing (epochē) the natural attitude. The method became the common starting point of continental thought.

Figures Husserl · Heidegger · Merleau-Ponty · Stein · Levinas

Existentialism

1930s — 1960s

Sartre, Beauvoir, and Camus held that “existence precedes essence”: the human being is whatever it makes of itself in a world without prescribed meaning, and is condemned to the freedom and responsibility of that making.

Figures Sartre · Simone de Beauvoir · Camus · Jaspers · Marcel

Analytic Philosophy

from 1900

Russell, Moore, the early Wittgenstein, and the Vienna Circle turned philosophy toward the logical analysis of language and the rigorous treatment of its traditional problems on the model of mathematics and the natural sciences.

Figures Russell · Moore · Wittgenstein · Carnap · Quine · Kripke

The Frankfurt School

from 1923

A program of “Critical Theory” combining Marx, Freud, and Hegel, devoted to diagnosing the pathologies of late-capitalist reason — most famously in Dialectic of Enlightenment's account of instrumental rationality consuming its own emancipatory promise.

Figures Horkheimer · Adorno · Marcuse · Benjamin · Habermas

Structuralism & After

1950s — 1980s

Saussure's linguistics opened onto a generation that read culture, kinship, the unconscious, and the history of thought as systems of differential signs — then, with Derrida and Foucault, into a sustained interrogation of the categories of the Western tradition itself.

Figures Lévi-Strauss · Lacan · Barthes · Foucault · Derrida · Deleuze

Anglophone Political Philosophy

from 1971

Rawls's A Theory of Justice revived the social-contract tradition for the post-war liberal democracies; Nozick replied from the libertarian right, MacIntyre and Sandel from a communitarian and virtue-ethical position.

Figures Rawls · Nozick · MacIntyre · Sandel · Walzer · Nussbaum
§ Key Concepts
Dasein
being-there

Heidegger's term for the kind of being that we ourselves are — the being for whom its own being is in question.

Bad Faith
mauvaise foi

Sartre's term for the lie one tells oneself in order to evade the anxiety of one's own radical freedom — pretending to be a fixed essence.

Language-Game
Sprachspiel

Late Wittgenstein's figure for the embedded, rule-governed practices in which language acquires its meanings — meaning as use.

Différance
deferral · difference

Derrida's neologism: meaning is never present in the sign but is endlessly deferred along chains of differential reference.

Veil of Ignorance
Rawls, 1971

The hypothetical device of choosing principles of justice without knowing what position one will occupy in the resulting society.

Biopower
biopouvoir

Foucault's name for the modern regime of power that takes life itself — populations, bodies, health, sexuality — as its object.
