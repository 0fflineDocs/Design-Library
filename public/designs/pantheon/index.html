The Greek Pantheon — Gods, Concepts & Creatures
Compendious Reference· Sixty-Two Entries· Five Tiers of Divinity

The Greeks did not invent a religion so much as inherit a quarrel — between an older order of cosmic powers and a younger family of bright, willful gods who deposed them. What follows is a compendium of that quarrel and of its long aftermath: the lineages, the offices, and the strange middle-creatures bred at its margins. Each entry collects the figure's domain, customary symbols, mythic kinships, and a brief description suitable for ready consultation.

The arrangement proceeds by age and stature, from the first Primordials born of Chaos, through the deposed Titans, to the twelve Olympians enthroned upon Mount Olympus, and at last to the lesser personifications and creatures of the bestiary. The reader is invited to wander as the eye is led.

Book I · Eight Entries

Primordials

The first powers, born of Chaos before the world was named

Before the order of the gods, before there was earth on which to set a temple or sky above to set a star, there was Chaos — a yawning, unshaped breach. From it the Primordials issued: not by birth, exactly, but by emergence, as fog rises out of marshland. They are less persons than principles. The world below was written in their hands before any other hand was raised against them.

Book II · Twelve Originals & Five Notables

The Titans

The elder gods, deposed by their own children

Twelve children sprang from the union of Ouranos and Gaia — six sons and six daughters, called the Titans. They ruled in a golden age until the youngest, Cronus, struck down his father and took the sky for himself; and were in turn struck down by their own son Zeus in the Titanomachy, a war ten years in length. The defeated were chained in Tartarus. A few — Themis, Mnemosyne, and the second generation — were spared and walked among the new gods.

Youngest of the Titans, he overthrew Ouranos and ruled until Zeus, the son he sought to swallow, deposed him in turn. His age was remembered as the golden one.

Book III · The Twelve

The Olympians

Enthroned upon Mount Olympus, the gods of the present age

After the Titanomachy the world was divided by lot among the three sons of Cronus: Zeus took the bright sky, Poseidon the salt sea, and Hades the world below. The earth and Olympus they held in common. About them gathered the company of twelve who feast on ambrosia in the cloud-girt halls — the gods to whom temples were raised in every Greek city and whose images are most familiar to us. They are bright, jealous, and often petty; their virtues and faults belong unmistakably to the people who imagined them.

ZeusΖεύς

JupiterIuppiter Optimus Maximus
King of the gods · god of the bright sky, thunder, hospitality, and oaths

Youngest son of Cronus and Rhea, hidden at birth and reared in the Cretan cave of Mount Ida by the nymph Amalthea. Having freed his swallowed brethren, he led the Olympians to victory in the ten-year war against the Titans, after which he was acknowledged king by lot of the sky. His justice is the justice of the storm — sudden, irreversible, and often partial.

The cuckoo is her courtship-bird; the peacock her watch-bird, its tail studded with the hundred eyes of Argus.

Book IV · Sixteen Entries

Personifications

The minor powers — concepts walked into the form of gods

Beyond the great gods stand a host of lesser figures whose names are also the names of nouns. They are the personifications: Victory, Sleep, Death, Strife, Rumour. The Greeks made gods of all of these and built no temples to most. Where the Olympians have a domain, the personifications are their domain — Nike does not preside over victory, she is victory, with wings.

Personification

Νίκη · Nike

Victoria
winged victory in any contest

Daughter of the Titan Pallas and the Styx; constant attendant of Athena and Zeus. She crowns the victor with a wreath of olive or laurel.

Personification

Θάνατος · Thanatos

Mors
gentle, inevitable death

Son of Nyx, twin of Hypnos. Not violent (that is Keres' work) but soft and final — the one who comes for the appointed soul at the appointed hour.

Personification

Ὕπνος · Hypnos

Somnus
sleep, that gentlest of brothers

Twin of Thanatos, dwelling in a cave on Lemnos through which Lethe flows. Father of the Oneiroi, the dreams that climb each night into mortal heads.

Personification

Νέμεσις · Nemesis

Invidia · Rivalitas
divine retribution against overweening pride

She of Rhamnous, who measures undeserved fortune and exacts the balance. The check on every too-easy victory and every too-confident king.

Personification

Ἔρις · Eris

Discordia
strife, quarrel, contention

Uninvited to the wedding of Peleus and Thetis, she threw among the gathered goddesses a golden apple inscribed for the fairest. From that quarrel came the war at Troy.

Personification

Τύχη · Tyche

Fortuna
fortune, the city's luck

She bears the cornucopia in one hand and the rudder of the world in the other; sometimes blind, sometimes wheeled. Every Hellenistic city had its own Tyche, crowned with its walls.

Personification

Ἶρις · Iris

Iris
the rainbow; messenger of the gods to mortals

Where Hermes carries Zeus's word among gods, Iris bears it from gods to men. Her path across the sky is the bow that follows rain.

Personification

Ἥβη · Hebe

Iuventas
youth in full bloom; cupbearer on Olympus

Daughter of Zeus and Hera. She poured the nectar of the gods until Ganymede took the office. Wife at last to the deified Heracles.

Goddess · Liminal

Ἑκάτη · Hecate

Trivia
witchcraft, the crossroads, ghosts, the moon by night

Three-formed and triple-faced. She holds two torches at the place where three roads meet, and the dead come at her call. The only daughter of Titans honoured by Zeus among the new gods.

God · Pastoral

Πάν · Pan

Faunus · Sylvanus
shepherds, mountain pastures, midday sleep, wild panic

Goat-legged son of Hermes, born so unsightly his nurse fled. The panic that empties an army at dusk is his — sudden, contagious, and reasonless.

God · Chthonic

Ἅιδης · Hades

Pluto · Dis Pater
the world below; lord of the dead and of buried wealth

Brother of Zeus and Poseidon, drawer of the third lot. He took Persephone by force; his realm is grave and just. The Greeks scarcely spoke his name and called him instead the Rich One.

Goddess · Chthonic

Περσεφόνη · Persephone

Proserpina · Kore
the queen of the underworld; spring's return

Daughter of Demeter, abducted while gathering narcissi. By eating six pomegranate seeds she bound herself to the world below for six months each year — and so the seasons turn.

Personification

Ἠώς · Eos

Aurora
the rosy-fingered dawn

Each morning she rises from her bed in Ocean and ascends to the sky to announce the coming of her brother Helios. She loved Tithonus and asked for his immortality, forgetting to ask for his youth.

Personification

Μοῖραι · The Moirai

Parcae · Fata
the three Fates; spinners of mortal lots

Clotho spins the thread, Lachesis measures it, Atropos cuts. To them even Zeus must defer; their judgments are the deepest in the order of things.

Personification

Ἀνάγκη · Ananke

Necessitas
inevitability; the compulsion that admits no plea

In Orphic teaching, she encircles the cosmos with her consort Chronos. Her arms are the iron bands by which what must happen happens.

Personification

Φόβος & Δεῖμος

Pavor · Metus
terror and dread; charioteers of Ares

Twin sons of Ares and Aphrodite who go before their father into battle and clear the field of resolve. Their faces are emblazoned upon the shields of war.

Book V · Twelve Creatures

Bestiary

Monstrous offspring of the gods, and the heroes who slew them

From the union of Echidna and Typhon — and from such other marriages as the world's margins permitted — came a brood of creatures unlike either gods or men. The heroes of the great age made their names by setting these creatures down: Heracles, Theseus, Bellerophon, Perseus. The bestiary that follows collects the most familiar, with a note on their lineage and on the fate that, in the end, found them.

Pegasus

the winged horse; bearer of the thunderbolt

Sprung from the severed neck of Medusa. Tamed by Bellerophon with Athena's golden bridle; carried him against the Chimera and at last carried him too high.

Minotaur

bull-headed son of Pasiphaë; eater of tribute

Hidden by Minos in the Labyrinth of Daedalus and fed each ninth year on seven Athenian youths and seven maidens, until Theseus slew him and found his way out by Ariadne's thread.

Lernaean Hydra

nine-headed marsh-serpent of Lerna

For each head Heracles severed two regrew, until Iolaus brought a torch and seared each wound. The middle head was immortal; he buried it under a rock.

Chimera

lion before, goat upon, serpent behind; breather of fire

Ravaged Lycia until Bellerophon, mounted on Pegasus, thrust a lead-tipped spear into its throat. The lead melted in its breath and choked it.

Cerberus

three-headed hound at the gates of Hades

He fawns on those entering and devours those who would leave. Heracles, in his twelfth labor, hauled him out into the daylight; Cerberus's spittle, falling, gave birth to aconite.

Sphinx

lion-bodied, woman-faced, winged; setter of riddles

Posted by Hera at the road to Thebes. She devoured those who could not answer her until Oedipus said man, and she threw herself from her rock.

Medusa

the mortal Gorgon; petrifier of those who meet her gaze

Once a priestess of Athena, transformed for her hubris into a serpent-haired terror. Perseus, looking only at her reflection in his shield, took her head; from her neck sprang Pegasus and Chrysaor.

Cyclopes

one-eyed giants; first smiths of the thunderbolt

Brontes, Steropes, and Arges — three brothers who forged for Zeus his bolts and for Poseidon his trident. A younger, savage kind dwelt on Sicilian shores; one of these was Polyphemus, blinded by Odysseus.

Centaurs

half-man, half-horse; the wild riders of Thessaly

Born of Ixion's lust upon a cloud. Most were drunken and violent — as their fight with the Lapiths attests — but Chiron, son of Cronus, was the wisest teacher in the world and tutor to Achilles.

Harpies

winged spirits of sudden storm and snatching wind

Daughters of Thaumas; sisters of Iris. They snatch food from the tables of the cursed (as they did from blind Phineus) and bear the souls of the lost to the Erinyes.

Typhon

hundred-headed serpent-storm; father of monsters

Last and worst child of Gaia, conceived in her rage at the fall of the Titans. He almost overthrew Zeus, who pinned him at last beneath Mount Etna, whose tremors are his.

Echidna

half-woman, half-serpent; mother of monsters

Bride of Typhon and dam to the Hydra, Cerberus, Chimera, Sphinx, and the Nemean Lion. Hesiod calls her the unwearying; she dwells in a cave below the earth.
